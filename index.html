Complete Drug Report

CLARITHROMYCIN
Clarithromycin, a 14-membered ring macrolide anti-biotic, is a derivative of erythromycin, with similar spectrum of activity and clinical uses, but more consistent absorption and a longer half-life.
Clarithromycin is a colorless, crystalline solid with a bitter taste. It is a semi-synthetic 14-membered ring macrolide antibiotic, prepared by the chemical modification of erythromycin A, to give the 6-methoxy derivative.! Erythromycin A itself is isolated from certain strains of Streptomyces erythreus. The absolute configuration of clarithromycin is as shown.
Solutions are levorotatory. It is not available in any combination preparations.

Medicine Name (Generic)


Clarithromycin

Molecular Formula


C38H69NO13
Structural Formula
Class
Macrolide

Molecular Weight


748.0 g/mol

Mechanism of Action


Inhibits the growth of bacteria.

Pharmacology


Clarithromycin works like erythromycin by binding to the 50S subunit of bacterial ribosomes, blocking protein synthesis. It doesn’t affect mammalian ribosomes. It penetrates well into white blood cells and macrophages. It targets most Gram-positive bacteria, some Gram-negative bacteria, chlamydiae, and mycoplasmas. It has no known direct pharmacological effects in mammals.

How to use this medicine?


Clarithromycin comes as a tablet, an extended-release (long-acting) tablet, and a suspension (liquid) to take by mouth. The regular tablet and liquid are usually taken with or without food every 8 (three times a day) to 12 hours (twice a day) for 7 to 14 days. The extended-release tablet is usually taken with food every 24 hours (once a day) for 7 to 14 days. Take clarithromycin at around the same time(s) every day.

What It’s Used For


Lower and upper respiratory tract infections: tonsillitis, pharyngitis, sinusitis, bronchitis, community acquired pneumonia. Skin and soft tissue infections, acute otitis media, mycobacterium avium infection

Other uses for this medicine


Clarithromycin also is used sometimes to treat other types of infections including Lyme disease (an infection that may develop after a person is bitten by a tick), cryptosporidiosis (an infection that causes diarrhea), cat scratch disease (an infection that may develop after a person is bitten or scratched by a cat), Legionnaires' disease, (type of lung infection), and pertussis (whooping cough; a serious infection that can cause severe coughing). It is also sometimes used to prevent heart infection in patients having dental or other procedures. Talk to your doctor about the possible risks of using this medication for your condition.

Allergies


Tell your doctor if you're allergic to Clarithromycin or similar antibiotics (like azithromycin or erythromycin).

Other Medicines


Some drugs and herbs (like St. John’s Wort or Prilosec) can affect Clarithromycin. Always share what you're taking with your doctor or pharmacist.

Health Conditions


Let your doctor know if you have liver problems, heart rhythm issues, muscle weakness, or low potassium/magnesium.

What should I do if I forget a dose?


Take the missed dose as soon as you remember it. However, if it is almost time for the next dose, skip the missed dose and continue your regular dosing schedule. Do not take a double dose to make up for a missed one.

Common Side Effects (usually mild – tell your doctor if they bother you or don’t go away)


● Diarrhea
●Nausea or vomiting
●Stomach pain or heartburn
●Gas or bloating
●Change in taste
●Headache
●Dizziness or confusion (avoid driving or using machines until you know how the medicine affects you)

Serious Side Effects

  • Stop taking the medicine and get medical help right away if you notice any of these:
  • Chest pain
  • Shortness of breath
  • Irregular/rapid heartbeat.
  • Severe or long-lasting diarrhea (watery or bloody, even weeks after treatment).
  • Yellowing of skin or eyes (a sign of liver problems).
  • Rash, hives, itching, or swelling (especially of the face, lips, tongue, or throat).
  • Trouble breathing or swallowing.
  • Blistering, peeling skin or flu-like symptoms.
  • Fever, extreme tiredness, or loss of appetite.
  • Dark urine or pain in upper right stomach.
  • Unusual bleeding, easy bruising, or constant fever/chills
  • Joint pain, muscle weakness, double vision, or trouble talking/chewing
  • Mouth sores or foul-smelling urine

  • Tablet:
  • Swallow the long-acting tablets whole; do not split, chew, or crush them.

Mode of use


The contents of a single vial are reconstituted with 10 ml sterile water which is then further diluted to a final concentration of 2 mg.ml-1 of fluid in 250 ml of 5% dextrose or normal saline solution) for infusion over 1 h. Reconstituted clarithromycin may be stored for up to 24h at 4°C.

Important


Concomitant administration with astemizole or terfenadine is not advised.

Administration


For IV administration only. 500 mg should be administered into one of the larger proximal veins as an IV infusion over 60 mins, using a solution concentration of about 2 mg/mL. Clarithromycin should not be given as a bolus or an IM injection.

Pharmacokinetics


Absorption: Immediate release:
Rapid; food delays rate, but not extent of absorption.
Metabolism: Partially hepatic; converted to 14-0H clarithromycin (active metabolite; undergoes extensive first-pass metabolism.
Bioavailability: Approx. 50%.
Half-life elimination: Immediate release: Clarithromycin: 3-7 hrs. 14-OH-clarithromycin: 5-9 hrs.
Time to peak: Immediate release: 2-3 hrs. Extended
Release: 5-8 hrs.
Excretion: Urine, feces.

Drug Interactions


Clarithromycin has numerous drug interactions, some potentially hazardous. Caution is advised with the followings:
1. Theophylline
Repeated use of clarithromycin may increase theophylline levels. Monitor serum concentrations during co-administration.
2. Warfarin
Clarithromycin inhibits warfarin metabolism, increasing bleeding risk. Monitor INR closely; fatal hemorrhage reported.
3. Carbamazepine
Increases AUC and half-life of carbamazepine. Monitor levels and consider dose adjustment.
4. Digoxin
Possible interaction due to P-glycoprotein inhibition. Monitor digoxin levels and watch for toxicity.
5. Astemizole & Terfenadine
Concomitant use can lead to QT prolongation and serious cardiac arrhythmias. Contraindicated.
6. Cisapride
Risk of QT prolongation and arrhythmia. Avoid co-administration.
7. Ergot Derivatives
May cause ergot toxicity (vasospasm, ischemia). Contraindicated.
8. Protease Inhibitors
Interaction through CYP3A4 inhibition may alter drug levels. Monitor for toxicity or reduced efficacy.
9. CYP3A4 Inducers
May reduce clarithromycin levels and effectiveness. Dose adjustment may be necessary.
10. Statins
Increased risk of statin toxicity (e.g., rhabdomyolysis), especially with simvastatin or lovastatin. Avoid or monitor closely.
11. Midazolam & Alprazolam
Enhanced sedative effects due to metabolism inhibition. Monitor for CNS depression.
12. Colchicine
Toxicity risk increased. Avoid or reduce colchicine dose.
13. Cyclosporin & Tacrolimus
Levels may increase; monitor blood concentrations and renal function.
14. PDE5 Inhibitors (e.g., Sildenafil)
Increased risk of hypotension and side effects. Use lower PDE5 inhibitor doses.
15. Calcium Channel Blockers
Potential hypotension and bradycardia due to elevated drug levels. Monitor blood pressure.
16. Aminoglycosides & Other Ototoxic Drugs
Risk of additive ototoxicity. Use with caution, especially in elderly or renal impairment.
17. Oral Hypoglycemics & Insulin
Risk of hypoglycemia. Monitor blood glucose levels closely.
18. Antiarrhythmics (e.g., Amiodarone)
QT prolongation risk. Avoid combination or monitor ECG closely.
19. Valproate
May alter valproate levels or toxicity profile. Monitor for clinical signs of interaction.
20. Atypical Antipsychotics
Risk of QT prolongation and increased antipsychotic levels. Monitor ECG and side effects.
21. Itraconazole
Increased levels of both drugs possible. Monitor for toxicity.
22. Rifabutin
Risk of increased rifabutin toxicity (e.g., uveitis). Monitor closely.
23. Sirolimus
Levels may rise. Therapeutic drug monitoring recommended.
24. NRTIs (e.g., Lamivudine, Abacavir)
Generally safe; minimal interaction, but monitor therapy effectiveness.
25. Eplerenone
Hyperkalemia risk due to CYP3A4 interaction. Avoid or monitor potassium closely.
26. Eletriptan
Avoid due to potential increased exposure and serotonin syndrome.
27. Cilostazol
Levels may rise; dose reduction of cilostazol may be needed.
28. Vinblastine
Risk of increased toxicity. Monitor for bone marrow suppression and neuropathy.
29. Methylprednisolone
May increase steroid levels. Monitor for corticosteroid-related side effects.
30. Omeprazole
Beneficial interaction—used in H. pylori eradication therapy.
31. Ticagrelor & Ranolazine
Risk of increased exposure. Avoid or use with caution; monitor for adverse effects.
32. Tolterodine
Increased exposure may occur. Dose adjustment may be needed in CYP2D6 poor metabolizers.
33. Oral Contraceptives
No significant effect on estrogen or progesterone levels. Safe to use together.
34. Zidovudine
Clarithromycin may reduce its absorption. Stagger doses to avoid interaction.

High-risk groups


These are the high-risk groups for clarithromycin use:
1. Neonates
May be used with appropriate dosing.
2. Breastfeeding Mothers
Clarithromycin is excreted into breast milk; caution advised due to infant exposure.
3. Children
Safe to use; recommended dose is 7.5 mg/kg every 12 hours.
4. Pregnant Women
Limited safety data; not recommended during pregnancy.
5. Elderly Patients
No special precautions needed if renal function is normal.
6. Severe Liver Disease
Metabolism is reduced; use with caution.
7. Severe Renal Failure
Reduce dose or extend dosing interval if creatinine clearance < 30 mL/min.

Storage and Handling
Avoid refrigerating oral suspension.
Keep in a cool, dry place, away from sunlight and moisture.
Keep out of reach of children.
Do not keep in the bathroom.
Keep it in its original container, tightly closed.
Do not put the liquid (suspension) in the fridge. Keep it at room temperature and throw away any leftover after 14 days.
Use safety caps and store in a high, locked place.

Patient Tips


Take sufficient amount of fluids (2-3 liters per day) to maintain hydration, unless restricted by the prescriber.
Take prescribed analgesic for headache or stomach cramps.

Other Important Information


If the victim has collapsed, had a seizure, has trouble breathing, or can't be awakened, immediately call emergency services at 911.
Do not share your medicine with anyone.
Your prescription might not be refillable—if you still feel sick after finishing it, call your doctor.
Keep a list of all the medicines, vitamins, and supplements you take.
Bring this list to doctor visits, hospital trips, or emergencies.
